What donating a kidney actually involves.

The whole process, in three parts. Most people who look at a tool like this are somewhere in the first part. That is the moment this tool is built for.

Part one You are probably here

Before you contact anyone

You learn that living kidney donation exists and wonder whether it could involve you. You read other donors' stories, look things up, and quietly work out how you feel about it. Wondering becomes weighing: would I do this? For whom? Under what conditions? What would stop me?

You have not contacted a transplant program yet. The friction here is internal, and there is no structured way to say what you actually think before the formal process starts. That is what WILD is for.

Part two

The medical path

If you decide to move forward, you contact a transplant hospital. Intake leads to initial screening, then a comprehensive two-day evaluation: labs, imaging, a psychosocial assessment, and a meeting with an Independent Living Donor Advocate whose only job is to look out for you. It is free to you; the recipient's insurance covers it.

At the end the team tells you whether surgery can be scheduled directly, whether a paired exchange is possible, or whether you are not eligible. This typically takes six to twelve months. If you are cleared, the final decision is yours, and you can withdraw at any time without the team telling your family why.

Part three

Surgery, recovery, and life after

The surgery is laparoscopic and takes roughly four hours. Most donors spend two or three nights in the hospital. Driving returns at about two weeks, normal activity at four to five, heavy lifting at six to twelve. Some donors report fatigue lasting longer than the benchmarks suggest.

Follow-up is required at six, twelve, and twenty-four months. After that you manage your health with your own doctor. Donors live normal lives. The most common long-term effect is a modest increase in blood-pressure risk, usually well controlled with medication. Perioperative mortality in the United States is about three in ten thousand.
