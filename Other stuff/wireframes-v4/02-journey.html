What does donating a kidney actually involve?

This page briefly describes the whole process, in three parts. Most people who look at a tool like this are somewhere in the first part, when they're thinking about donation early on.

Part one You are probably here

Thinking about donating a kidney

Everyone starts here. A person hears about kidney donation, maybe because their loved one needs a kidney transplant. People learn that living kidney donation exists and wonder whether it could involve them.

The process of working through that is different for everyone, but ultimately the question is: would I do this? For whom? Under what conditions? What would stop me? There are many factors to consider and the point of this app is to help you work through them so you know which ones matter to your choices.

Part two

Evaluating your eligibility

If you decide to move forward, you contact a transplant center, where you will undergo initial screening for common medical issues that would make donation inadvisable. From there, a comprehensive evaluation takes place that involves labs, imaging, a psychosocial assessment, and - depending on your center - a meeting with an Independent Living Donor Advocate whose only job is to look out for you. This evaluation process is free to you because the recipient's insurance covers it.

At the end the team tells you whether surgery can be scheduled directly, whether a paired exchange is possible, or whether you are not eligible. This can take several months six to twelve months. If you are cleared, the final decision is yours, and you can withdraw at any time without the transplant team telling anyone why you withdrew. You can always come back later if you change your mind.

Part three

Surgery, recovery, and life after

The surgery is laparoscopic and takes roughly four hours. Most donors spend two or three nights in the hospital. Donors can typically return to driving at about two weeks, normal activity at four to five, and heavy lifting at six to twelve. Some donors report fatigue lasting longer than the benchmarks suggest, so be aware every donor is different.

Follow-up visits are generally required at six, twelve, and twenty-four months. After that you manage your health with your own doctor. While there are some increased long-term health risks, donors usually live normal, healthy lives. The most common long-term effect is a modest increase in blood-pressure risk, usually well controlled with medication. Perioperative mortality in the United States is about three in ten thousand, so it's a rare, but not impossible, event.
